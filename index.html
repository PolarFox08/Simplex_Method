Chapter 1
The Factory Problem
Before any math — let's understand what we're actually solving.
🏭
You run a small furniture factory. You make chairs (x₁) and tables (x₂). Your goal: maximize total profit.
🪑 Chairs (x₁)

Each chair earns $3 profit. Relatively quick to make.

🪑 Tables (x₂)

Each table earns $5 profit. More valuable but uses more wood.

Physical Constraints
🪵 Wood Constraint
x₁ + 2x₂ ≤ 8

You have 8 units of wood. A chair uses 1 unit, a table uses 2 units. Tables are more wood-intensive.

⏱ Labor Constraint
x₁ + x₂ ≤ 5

You have 5 labor-hours. Each product (chair or table) takes exactly 1 hour to make.

The Objective
Maximize Z = 3x₁ + 5x₂

The Simplex Method will walk us from "produce nothing" → the most profitable corner of the feasible region, step by step.

Chapter 2
Standard Form: Adding Slack Variables
The Simplex Method needs equalities, not inequalities. We convert by adding slack variables — variables that represent unused resources.
Wood Constraint
x₁ + 2x₂ ≤ 8
↓ add s₁
x₁ + 2x₂ + s₁ = 8
What is s₁?
  • s₁ = units of wood NOT used (leftover wood)
  • If x₁=0, x₂=0: s₁ = 8 — all wood is idle
  • If x₂=2 tables: s₁ = 8 − 4 = 4 — 4 units still unused
  • s₁ earns $0 profit — idle resources earn nothing
Labor Constraint
x₁ + x₂ ≤ 5
↓ add s₂
x₁ + x₂ + s₂ = 5
What is s₂?
  • s₂ = labor-hours NOT used (idle labor)
  • If nothing is produced: s₂ = 5 — all hours idle
  • If 3 products are made: s₂ = 5 − 3 = 2
  • s₂ earns $0 profit — idle workers earn nothing
Complete System in Standard Form
Maximize: Z = 3x₁ + 5x₂ + 0s₁ + 0s₂ Subject to: x₁ + 2x₂ + s₁ = 8 x₁ + x₂ + s₂ = 5 x₁, x₂, s₁, s₂ ≥ 0
💡
We now have 4 variables and 2 constraints. At any corner point, exactly 2 variables are active (in the basis) and 2 are zero. The Simplex Method moves between corners.

All zeros because C_B is all zeros. We earn nothing now, so we lose nothing by switching.

Step C — Iteration 0
Ratio Test: Finding the Bottleneck
Divide each row's Quantity by the x₂ pivot column value (only positive values). The smallest ratio tells us which resource runs out first.
C_BBasisZonex₁x₂ (pivot)s₁s₂QtyRatio: Qty ÷ x₂
0s₁Wood 12108 8 ÷ 2 = 4
0s₂Labor 11015 5 ÷ 1 = 5
Row 1 (Wood) 8 units of wood ÷ 2 wood per table = 4 tables max 👑 SMALLEST
Row 2 (Labor) 5 labor-hours ÷ 1 hour per table = 5 tables max
⛏️
Minimum ratio = 4 (Row 1 wins). Wood runs out first — after making 4 tables, all 8 wood units are gone (4 × 2 = 8). We still have labor left, but wood stops us at 4 tables.
Pivot Row

Row 1 (s₁ row) — the wood constraint is the bottleneck.

Pivot Element

2 — the value at the intersection of x₂ column and s₁ row. Means each table uses 2 wood.

Exchange

s₁ leaves (wood fully used), x₂ enters. Move from (0,0) → (0,4).

Step D — Iteration 0
Row Operations: Pivoting
Two operations transform the tableau. Goal: make the pivot column have a 1 in the pivot row and 0 everywhere else.
1

Normalize the Pivot Row — Divide all elements of Row 1 by the pivot element (2). This makes the pivot element = 1.

New Row 1 = Old Row 1 ÷ 2
Old Row 1: [ 1, 2, 1, 0 | 8 ]
÷ 2 ÷ 2 ÷ 2 ÷ 2 ÷ 2
New Row 1: [ 1/2, 1, 1/2, 0 | 4 ]
💬
Reading New Row 1: x₂ = 4. The RHS = 4 is our key result — we can make exactly 4 tables. The ½ under x₁ means making 1 chair costs ½ table of wood capacity.
2

Eliminate x₂ from Row 2 — Row 2 has a 1 in the x₂ column. Subtract 1 × New Row 1 from Old Row 2 to make it 0.

New Row 2 = Old Row 2 − (1) × New Row 1
Old Row 2: [ 1, 1, 0, 1 | 5 ]
− (1) × New R1: [ 1/2, 1, 1/2, 0 | 4 ]
New Row 2: [ 1/2, 0, -1/2, 1 | 1 ]
✅
Verify the RHS: 4 tables use 4 labor-hours. We had 5. So 1 labor-hour is still idle — exactly what the new Row 2 RHS = 1 shows. The algebra checks out!

Iteration 1 — Deep Dive
Understanding the Body Coefficients
Every number in the updated body has a precise physical meaning — let's unpack them all.
Row 1: x₂ row (Wood/Table row)
  • ½ under x₁: Introducing 1 chair reduces table production by ½. Why? A chair uses 1 wood. Each wood unit is worth ½ table (tables use 2 wood each). So: 1 chair = ½ fewer tables via wood.
  • 1 under x₂: Identity — x₂ is basic in this row.
  • ½ under s₁: Every unit of wood we leave idle costs ½ table of production. Idling wood is expensive!
  • 0 under s₂: Idle labor doesn't affect the wood/table row directly.
  • RHS = 4: We are currently making 4 tables.
Row 2: s₂ row (Labor row)
  • ½ under x₁: Each chair uses 1 labor-hour directly, BUT making a chair also displaces ½ table (wood row), and each dropped table frees ½ labor-hour. Net = 1 − ½ = ½ hour per chair. Chairs are cheaper in labor than they seem!
  • 0 under x₂: x₂ was fully substituted out — tables no longer appear in the labor row explicitly.
  • −½ under s₁: Leaving 1 more wood idle → make ½ fewer tables → free up ½ labor-hour. So more idle wood leads to more idle labor (they move together).
  • 1 under s₂: Identity — s₂ is basic here.
  • RHS = 1: 1 labor-hour is still idle. After making 4 tables (using 4 hours), 1 hour remains unused.
🔬
Shadow Price of Wood: Z(s₁) = 2.5, so Cⱼ−Zⱼ(s₁) = −2.5. This means each unit of wood we idle costs us $2.50 of profit. Turning this around: each additional unit of wood is worth $2.50 to our factory.

  • 1 under s₁: If we relax wood (+1 unit), we gain 1 more table. Shadow price: 1 table per wood unit.
